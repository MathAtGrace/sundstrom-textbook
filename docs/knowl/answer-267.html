Answer 3.1.

Let \(u \in A \times \left( {B \cap C} \right)\text{.}\) Then, there exists \(x \in A\) and there exists \(y \in B \cap C\) such that \(u = \left( {x, y} \right)\text{.}\) Since \(y \in B \cap C\text{,}\) we know that \(y \in B\) and \(y \in C\text{.}\) So, we have:

\(u = \left( x, y \right) \in A \times B\) and \(u = \left( x, y \right) \in A \times C\text{.}\)
Hence, \(u \in \left( A \times B \right) \cap \left( A \times C \right)\) and this proves that \(A \times \left( {B \cap C} \right) \subseteq \left( A \times B \right) \cap \left( A \times C \right)\text{.}\) Now let \(v \in \left( A \times B \right) \cap \left( A \times C \right)\text{.}\) Then, \(v \in A \times B\) and \(v \in A \times C\text{.}\) So, there exists an \(s\) in \(A\) and a \(t\) in \(B\) such that \(v = \left( s, t \right)\text{.}\) But, since \(v\) is also in \(A \times C\text{,}\) we see that \(t\) must also be in \(C\text{.}\) Thus, \(t \in B \cap C\) and so, \(v \in A \times \left( B \cap C \right)\text{.}\) This proves that \(\left( A \times B \right) \cap \left( A \times C \right) \subseteq A \times \left( B \cap C \right)\text{.}\)
