Task 12.d.

In advanced calculus, a sequence of real numbers \(\left(x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_k, \ldots \right)\) is called a Cauchy sequence provided that for each positive real number \(\varepsilon\text{,}\) there exists a natural number \(N\) such that for all \(m, n \in \N\text{,}\) if \(m > N\) and \(n > N\text{,}\) then \(\left| x_n - x_m \right| \lt \varepsilon\text{.}\) Carefully explain what it means to say that the sequence of real numbers \(\left(x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_k, \ldots \right)\) is not a Cauchy sequence.
