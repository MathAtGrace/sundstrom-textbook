For the inductive step, we prove that for all \(k \in \mathbb{N}\text{,}\) if \(P \left( k \right)\) is true, then \(P \left( k + 1 \right)\) is true. So let \(k\) be a natural number and assume that \(P( k )\) is true. That is, assume that

\begin{equation*} 4\text{ divides } \left( {5^k - 1} \right)\text{.} \end{equation*}

This means that there exists an integer \(m\) such that

\begin{equation*} 5^k - 1 = 4m\text{.} \end{equation*}

Thus,

\begin{equation*} 5^k = 4m + 1 \qquad (1)\text{.} \end{equation*}

In order to prove that \(P( k + 1 )\) is true, we must show that 4 divides \(\left( {5^{k + 1} - 1} \right)\text{.}\) Since \(5^{k + 1} = 5 \cdot 5^k\text{,}\) we can write

\begin{equation*} 5^{k + 1} - 1 = 5 \cdot 5^k - 1 \qquad (2)\text{.} \end{equation*}

We now substitute the expression for \(5^k\) from equation (1) into equation (2). This gives

\begin{align*} 5^{k + 1} - 1 \amp = 5 \cdot 5^k - 1 \amp\\ \amp = 5( {4m + 1} ) - 1 \amp\\ \amp = ( {20m + 5} ) - 1 \amp\\ \amp = 20m + 4 \amp\\ \amp = 4( {5m + 1} ) \amp (3) \end{align*}
